What is this course, and why nonstandard?

Course description and motivation.

Course description

  • Title: Math 000 – Nonstandard Calculus I
  • Prerequisite: Math 150 (Precalculus) or equivalent.
  • Requirement: Fulfills the Calculus I requirement (Math 131, 142, 147, 151, 171).
  • Together with Math 001 Nonstandard Calculus II, this sequence covers the conceptual content of the regular two-course Calculus I–II sequence.

Why a nonstandard approach?

Traditional calculus courses often present limits and infinity in a way that feels distant from how students informally think about “very small” and “very large.” By using the framework of hyperreal numbers and infinitesimals, this course allows us to talk about infinitely small and infinitely large quantities more directly. This approach follows the spirit of the original formulations of calculus by Newton and Leibniz and can reduce the number of separate definitions and notations students must juggle.

Teaching philosophy in this course

I take seriously Plutarch’s idea that the mind is not a container to be filled but a fire to be kindled. Instead of viewing lectures as a one-way transfer of information, I will ask you to think actively, make conjectures, and discover key ideas for yourself. Our aim is not just to memorize theorems, but to understand how and why they work and to explain them to others.

Learning outcomes

What you should be able to do by the end of the course.

In line with the idea that students must own their own goals, I invite you to adopt the following outcomes and adapt them to your personal aspirations for the course:

  • Develop a working understanding of the hyperreal number system and how infinitesimals model “very small” quantities.
  • Reason carefully about continuity and differentiability using nonstandard methods.
  • Use differentiation techniques to solve a variety of conceptual and applied problems.

Course policies

Homework, quizzes, participation, and exams.

Homework

Homework policy

  • Assigned weekly and submitted electronically (Canvas) by 11:59 PM on the due date.
  • Accepted up to one week late with a 5% deduction per day.
  • Collaboration is encouraged, but each student submits their own write-up.
  • You are encouraged to follow Francis Su’s guidelines for writing mathematics to make your work clear and readable.
Quizzes

Quiz policy

  • Short quizzes will typically occur every other Monday.
  • Quizzes focus on material from the preceding two weeks and are meant to check that you can work independently without aids.
  • The lowest quiz score will be dropped.
Participation

Participation policy

  • Active engagement in class is expected, including discussions and problem-solving.
  • I may call on students at random to share ideas or work through a problem at the board.
  • Participation is graded based on involvement in activities, not on having perfect answers.
Exams

Exam policy

  • One in-class midterm exam will be given on October 17.
  • The final exam is scheduled for December 12, 10:30 AM – 12:30 PM.

  • Make-up exams are only offered in exceptional circumstances; please contact me as soon as possible if something arises.

Weekly topics (tentative)

Subject to minor adjustments as needed.

  • Week 1: Zeno’s paradoxes and a brief history of calculus.
  • Week 2: Rates of change.
  • Week 3: Hyperreal numbers and infinitesimals.
  • Week 4: Functions and continuity.
  • Week 5: Properties of continuous functions.
  • Week 6: Introduction to derivatives.
  • Week 7: Properties of derivatives.
  • Week 8: Review and midterm exam.
  • Week 9: Geometric interpretation of derivatives.
  • Week 10: Increasing/decreasing behavior and local extrema.
  • Week 11: Optimization problems.
  • Week 12: Implicit differentiation and related rates.
  • Week 13: Higher-order derivatives.
  • Week 14: L’Hôpital’s rule and related techniques.
  • Week 15: Review and final exam.
