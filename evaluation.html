Teaching Self-Evaluation

EDAD 601 - College Teaching

Reflecting on a lesson about obstacles to higher-order thinking and thinking frameworks.

This page summarizes my self-evaluation after teaching a lesson in EDAD 601. It combines quantitative feedback (Likert-scale items) with qualitative comments and concrete steps I am taking to improve.

Lesson snapshot

What happened, and what I noticed in the moment.

The lesson centered on obstacles to higher-order thinking, frameworks for thinking, and strategies to improve thinking quality. I experimented with a random student picker right at the start, calling on Winn for the first question. Her visible discomfort and my own hesitation in responding showed me immediately how powerful and risky this tool could be. Simon, another classmate, stepped in to answer, and the lesson continued, but that moment framed much of my later reflection on participation and safety.

This was my first time delivering a full lesson rather than a short presentation, and I was still tweaking the plan up to the last minute. In hindsight, I see both the value of careful lesson planning and the importance of leaving space for improvisation and student needs.

Quantitative feedback

Likert-scale responses and what they suggest.

I began my reflection with the numbers. The table below summarizes the mean, median, and variance for each item on the feedback form (scale 1-5):

Item Mean Median Variance
Organized Presentation 3.81 4 1.77
Knowledgeable 4.57 5 2.06
Communicated Clearly 3.38 3 1.15
Class Participation 3.90 4 1.54
Increased Understanding 3.76 4 1.78

The clearest message is that my subject-matter knowledge came across very strongly (mean ≈ 4.57, median = 5). At the same time, “Communicated Clearly” has the lowest mean (≈ 3.38) and also the smallest variance, which suggests that concerns about clarity are fairly consistent across students, not just outliers.

The other items, organization, participation, and increased understanding, cluster around a median of 4 but with relatively large variances, indicating that many students had positive experiences while a smaller group did not. This tells me that the structure of the lesson worked well for some, but not yet reliably for everyone.

Qualitative feedback

What students said worked well, and what did not.

What worked

  • Use of a website for the lesson: most students appreciated having the material on a website rather than traditional slides, and liked the structure it provided.
  • Community-built obstacle list: students valued collaboratively constructing a list of obstacles to higher-order thinking instead of being handed a pre-made list.
  • Hearing thought processes, not just answers: some students enjoyed seeing peers explain how they arrived at answers, especially when the random selector called on them.

What needs work

  • Website design and readability: there was often too much text on the screen at once and the font size was small, making it hard to follow.
  • Random student picker: some found it exciting and fair, while others felt it was stressful, forced, and occasionally unkind to unprepared students.
  • Communication style: multiple comments pointed to the need for a stronger voice, fewer filler words, and more deliberate movement and eye contact.

How I am responding

Concrete changes I am making based on this feedback.

Improving communication

To address the concerns about clarity and presence, I have begun attending a free online improvisational comedy course (Radical Agreement). Improv gives structured practice in speaking spontaneously, listening closely, and committing to what you say without hedging every sentence. I expect this to help with both my verbal clarity and my confidence in front of a class.

For physical presence, I am experimenting with a remote control for navigating my teaching website. If I can move through pages and sections by remote control, I can spend less time at the podium and more time moving around the room and making eye contact.

Rethinking the random selector

I still believe there is value in tools that spread participation more evenly, but I need to use them more thoughtfully. Based on student suggestions, future modifications include:

  • Allowing a student to pass and then opening the question to the entire class.
  • Selecting a small group of students and letting them choose which question they want to answer.
  • Not permanently removing selected students from the pool, so no one can mentally “check out” after being called once.

Designing the website for readability

I plan to restructure my teaching pages so that each screen contains less text, with larger fonts and more visual hierarchy. Since a website is more customizable than static slides, I can use collapsible sections, better spacing, and interactive elements rather than long paragraphs of text.

Learning objectives: where I met them and where I fell short

Evaluating the lesson against my own goals.

Objective 1 - Obstacles to higher-order thinking (mostly successful)

Students were able to generate a rich list of obstacles and connect them to their own experiences. The open-ended comments suggest that they appreciated both the individual reflections and the class-wide synthesis.

Objective 2 - Thinking frameworks (partially successful)

Asking students to identify and contrast different thinking frameworks had mixed results. Some liked having definitions to anchor the more open discussion, but many felt the activity lacked context—some found it too easy, others too hard. In hindsight, I focused so much on not simply “giving away” the answer that I did not set up the question properly.

One suggestion I want to adopt is to frame these frameworks with stories and historical context. Rather than dropping labels out of nowhere, I can connect them to specific people, classroom situations, or historical shifts in educational thinking.

Objective 3 - Strategies to improve thinking (largely unsuccessful)

I ran out of time before we could meaningfully tackle strategies for improving thinking quality. This was less about student engagement and more about my own time management: I spent too long on earlier segments and tried to cover too much in one lesson.

Going forward, I intend to budget time more deliberately, identify which parts of a lesson are truly essential, and be willing to skip or shorten less critical elements to protect space for application and synthesis.

Closing reflection

Why this experience makes me want to keep teaching.

This was my first experience teaching a full lesson to a classroom of peers, and it was both frightening and energizing. I saw very clearly how my experiments could create stress as well as engagement, and how much I still have to learn about pacing, presence, and clarity. At the same time, I was struck by how supportive my classmates were, both in the moment and in their written feedback.

Writing this evaluation has convinced me even more that teaching is a craft I want to keep working on. I made mistakes, but they are mistakes I can learn from, and I am excited to try again with these lessons in mind.
