Teaching Philosophy

Teaching to Learn

A view of teaching as shared problem solving, where mistakes and growth matter more than quick correctness.

This page is adapted from my teaching philosophy statement written for EDAD 601 College Teaching. It reflects my background in engineering and mathematics, and how that shapes my approach to the classroom.

Learning and teaching are inseparable

Why I care not just that students learn, but that they learn to teach.

I often see the slogan “What is important is learning, not teaching.” At first glance this sounds noble, but I find it incomplete. Learning never happens in a vacuum: it always involves some form of teaching, whether from an instructor, a peer, a book, or the learner talking themselves through an idea.

Historically, English even used “learn” in the sense of “to teach,” and many scientists and educators have emphasized that you only really understand something when you can explain it clearly to someone else. In that spirit, my goal is not just that students learn content, but that they learn to teach what they have learned, to a classmate, a younger sibling, or a parent at the dinner table.

Practically, this means I design activities where students summarize a concept in their own words, talk through a problem solution to a partner, or prepare a short “mini-explanation” to share with the class. When students take on the role of teacher, even briefly, they confront gaps in their understanding and build confidence in their voice.

Being wrong is what makes being right meaningful

Creating a classroom where error is a resource, not a failure.

I resonate with the idea that teachers can and do make mistakes. What matters is not pretending to be infallible, but modeling how to respond when we are wrong. For me, error is not just a liability to be minimized; it is a central part of the learning process.

In mathematics education, George Pólya argued that students should be encouraged to guess, explore, and revise their thinking. Wrong answers are not simply discarded; they are examined: what part of the reasoning was insightful, where did it go astray, and how can it be repaired? I try to adopt this stance in my own teaching. Rather than rushing to the “official” solution, I like to put several student ideas on the board and work through them, highlighting both their strengths and their flaws.

This approach feels even more urgent now that AI systems can quickly produce correct answers. If correctness alone is the goal, a machine will usually be faster. A human classroom adds value when it cultivates curiosity, resilience, and the habit of reasoning out loud. I want students to see that thoughtful mistakes are not something to be ashamed of; they are the raw material out of which understanding is built.

One analogy I draw from my engineering background is overfitting: a model that perfectly matches past data may fail utterly on new situations. In the same way, students who only memorize polished solutions may struggle when faced with a novel problem. A classroom that tolerates and analyzes error helps them develop knowledge that generalizes beyond the specific homework set or exam.

Assessment through challenge and growth

Grading not just what students can do now, but how far they have come.

Because I see learning as a process of exploration, I am wary of grading systems that reward only quick correctness on routine exercises. Instead, I am drawn to assessments built around very challenging problems, tasks that students cannot immediately solve, but can gradually approach as their understanding deepens.

In such a system, a student’s final grade is not determined solely by their absolute score on a fixed set of hard problems. Rather, it reflects how much progress they have made relative to where they began. A student who starts out unable to make any headway on a difficult proof but, by the end of the semester, can outline a strategy and justify key steps should be recognized for that growth, even if their work is still imperfect.

Concretely, I imagine structures like:

  • A sequence of “challenge problems” revisited multiple times during the course, with students submitting improved attempts and reflections.
  • Rubrics that explicitly separate current level of mastery from degree of improvement, so a student can earn credit for meaningful progress.
  • Opportunities for revision: students can rework earlier solutions after feedback, demonstrating how their thinking has evolved.

This growth-oriented assessment signals to students that struggling with a hard question is not a sign that they do not “belong” in mathematics. Instead, it is an expected and valued part of the course. I want grades to tell a story not just about where students ended up, but about the journey they took to get there.

Education as a shared journey

Teacher and students learning from each other.

Ultimately, I see education as a shared journey rather than a one-way transfer of information. My role as a teacher is not to act as an unerring authority, but as a guide who creates conditions for exploration and dialogue. Students, in turn, are not passive recipients; they are active participants who question, explain, test ideas, and help each other learn.

When I invite students to teach small pieces of the course to one another, when I treat wrong answers as starting points for inquiry, and when I assess them based on their growth on challenging tasks, I am trying to align my practice with this belief. In doing so, I also hold myself accountable: if I want students to learn deeply, I must keep learning how to teach more thoughtfully.

In that sense, my teaching philosophy is also a learning philosophy. I teach because I want to understand better; I ask students to explain because I want them to understand better. The classroom works best when everyone in it, students and instructor alike, are both teachers and learners.
