Frameworks that Support Teaching Thinking

Bloom → Anderson & Krathwohl (Revised)

Bloom’s Taxonomy and the Revision Original (Bloom et al., 1956): knowledge → comprehension → application → analysis → synthesis → evaluation. Revised (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001): remember → understand → apply → analyze → evaluate → create. Shifts nouns → verbs; places “create” above “evaluate.” Encourages measurable learning objectives and higher-order test design.

Perry’s Scheme

Dualism: knowledge as right/wrong. Multiplism: acceptance of multiple opinions all equal. Relativism: awareness that context matters and certain knowledge claims are better for different purposes. Commitment: forming and sticking to one's beliefs while remaining open to new evidence.

SOLO Taxonomy

Prestructural – isolated facts. Unistructural – grasp of one idea. Multistructural – many ideas, little integration. Relational – connections form coherent whole. Extended abstract – transfer and generalization. Encourages deep learning assessment over rote memorization.

Sternberg’s Triarchic Intelligence

Analytic intelligence: abstraction, analysis. Creative intelligence: innovation, novelty management. Practical intelligence: applying learning to real contexts. Teachers can diversify assignments to tap each domain.

Kolb’s Experiential Cycle

Concrete experience – hands-on, emotional engagement. Reflective observation – careful analysis and judgment. Abstract conceptualization – theorizing and logical reasoning. Active experimentation – hypothesis testing.

Emotion & Cognition

Rejecting Cartesian dualism, Damasio (1995) argues in Descartes’ Error that emotion and reason are inseparable. Positive affect enhances persistence and insight. Negative affect (e.g., anxiety) can block learning. Teachers should normalize emotion and monitor classroom affect.

Threshold Concepts

Meyer & Land (2006) identify threshold concepts as transformative ideas that reorganize understanding within a discipline, turning confusion (“troublesome knowledge”) into insight. Instructors should prioritize these key concepts over exhaustive coverage. Once grasped, such ideas reshape learners’ worldview and self-concept.
