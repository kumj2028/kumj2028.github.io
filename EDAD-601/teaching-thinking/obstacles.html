What Are We Up Against?

Study Spotlight: "They Don't Read Very Well" (Carlson et al., 2024)

Abstract: This paper analyzes the results from a think-aloud reading study designed to test the reading comprehension skills of 85 English majors from two regional Kansas universities. From January to April of 2015, subjects participated in a recorded, twenty-minute reading session in which they were asked to read the first seven paragraphs of Charles Dickens' Bleak House out loud to a facilitator and then translate each sentence into plain English. Before subjects started the reading tests, they were given access to online resources and dictionaries and advised that they could also use their own cell phones as a resource. The facilitators also assured the subjects that were free to go at their own pace and did not have to finish reading all seven paragraphs by the end of the exam. As part of the study, each subject filled out a survey collecting personal data (class rank, G.P.A., etc.) and took a national literacy exam (the Degrees of Reading Power Test 10A). After the 85 taped reading tests were completed, the results were transcribed and coded.

Results:

58% (49/85) were classified as problematic: they grasped too little of the passage to read the novel unaided (though most believed they could); 38% (32/85) were competent: they caught more vocabulary and some figures of speech but only about half the literal prose; and 5% (4/85) were proficient: they tracked clauses closely and built accurate literal interpretations.

Reading Proficiency vs. DRP Scores:

Linking the DRP to performance, everyone scoring under 79% on the DRP ended up problematic on Dickens; yet a majority (59%) of the problematic group had DRP scores above 90%, indicating that strong general reading scores didn't guarantee success with complex literary prose.

Suggested obstacles (from the chapter)

Click to compare your list with a suggested set.

  • Limited practice: Prior schooling often rewards memorization rather than reasoning.
  • Limited time: Students optimize for efficiency unless thinking is explicitly valued.
  • Limited patience: Open-ended tasks feel ambiguous and frustrating at first.
  • Limited direction: Novices rarely see expert thinking modeled explicitly.
  • Limited expectations: Many underestimate the relevance of deep thinking to careers.

Implication: Build time, credit, and clarity for thinking itself—not just for coverage.
