Lesson Plan

Student Cognitive Development

EDAD 601 College Teaching · 1-hour lesson on thinking, frameworks, and obstacles.

A structured plan for a class session on student cognitive development, focusing on obstacles to higher-order thinking, major thinking frameworks, and strategies to improve the quality of students’ thinking.

Lesson overview

Context, timing, and materials.

  • Course: EDAD 601 – College Teaching
  • Instructor: Mengxiang Jiang
  • Date: October 31, 2025
  • Title: Student Cognitive Development
  • Time required: 1 hour
  • Required materials:
    • Computer with internet access
    • Projector displaying the computer screen to the class

This lesson plan was originally drafted by following the structure suggested in the Wikipedia article on lesson plans and then adapted to the specific needs of EDAD 601.

Learning objectives

What students should be able to do by the end of the lesson.

  • Identify common obstacles that impede higher-order thinking for students.
  • Identify and contrast several frameworks for thinking and cognitive development (e.g., Bloom, Anderson & Krathwohl, Perry, SOLO).
  • Generate strategies to improve the quality of students’ thinking in college classrooms.

Set (Opening)

Hooking students and framing the lesson.

Begin by showing the study “They Don’t Read Very Well”, which describes a mismatch between standardized literacy test scores and students’ ability to carry out an actual reading task. Ask students:

  • What went wrong in this study?
  • How does this relate to critical thinking in our own classrooms?

Use the discussion to motivate the rest of the lesson: high scores and apparent success do not guarantee deep understanding or effective thinking.

Instructional sequence

Step-by-step flow of the lesson.

  1. Obstacles to critical thinking (whole-class brainstorm)
    Immediately after the opening study, use a random student selection method to invite several students (e.g., four) to name an obstacle that prevented critical thinking in the scenario. Record their responses where everyone can see them.
    Then, show the list of obstacles from the textbook and compare them to the class-generated list.
  2. Po-Shen Loh video on problem solving
    Show a short video of Po-Shen Loh (coach of the U.S. IMO team) emphasizing the importance of thinking about how to solve a problem rather than expecting to be shown the method. Use this as a springboard to discuss students’ expectations of instruction versus their own responsibility for thinking.
  3. Bloom’s original taxonomy → Anderson & Krathwohl revision
    Present nouns from Bloom’s original taxonomy of cognitive levels. Randomly select students to propose appropriate action verbs that might correspond to each level. After collecting suggestions, reveal the verbs from the revised taxonomy of Anderson and Krathwohl and compare:
    • Where do the students’ verbs align with the revised taxonomy?
    • Where do they differ, and what does that say about how they view learning?
  4. Perry’s scheme
    Introduce key terms from Perry’s scheme for epistemological development (e.g., dualism, multiplicity, relativism, commitment). Randomly select students to guess what each term might mean and how it might relate to Bloom’s taxonomy.
    Facilitate a short discussion on how cognitive level and epistemological stance interact in the classroom.
  5. SOLO taxonomy
    Present the main levels of the SOLO taxonomy. Again, randomly select students to infer meanings of the terms and then compare with any previous frameworks discussed. Highlight similarities and differences among Bloom, Perry, and SOLO.
  6. Other frameworks and learning styles
    Briefly touch on additional frameworks mentioned in the textbook. Note in particular the limited empirical support behind many learning style models and the risks of over-interpreting them.
  7. Strategies for encouraging critical thinking
    Using random selection, ask students to suggest practical strategies instructors could use to encourage critical thinking in their own courses. Collect a list of takeaways and then compare it to the guidelines presented in the textbook.

Independent / group practice (time permitting)

Optional activity if there is time at the end of the lesson.

If time allows, show an excerpt from Steven Krantz’s How to Teach Mathematics that describes the Moore method (students proving results themselves rather than being shown proofs).

Ask students to form groups of 4-5 and discuss:

  • What are the advantages of the Moore method for cognitive development?
  • What are potential disadvantages or risks?
  • What kinds of students or classrooms might benefit most from this approach?

Have a few groups share their insights with the whole class to surface different perspectives on this high-challenge teaching method.

Summary & closing

Reinforcing the key ideas.

Conclude by revisiting the key points:

  • Obstacles to higher-order thinking are common, and recognizing them is the first step toward addressing them.
  • Different frameworks (Bloom, Anderson & Krathwohl, Perry, SOLO) offer complementary ways to think about student learning and development.
  • Instructors can actively design activities and environments that push students to think more deeply, not just perform well on tests.

Return briefly to the opening study, emphasizing that strong performance on a standardized test did not predict success on the authentic reading task. Encourage students to cultivate habits of asking why an answer is correct, not only what the answer is.

Evaluation, risks, and continuity

How the lesson will be assessed and how it fits into the course.

Evaluation

Planned evaluation

The original plan was to build a short multiple-choice quiz on the companion website so students could check their understanding of key concepts (frameworks, obstacles, and strategies). This quiz was not completed in time for the lesson, but remains a future improvement.

Qualitative evaluation will take place through class discussion, informal checks for understanding, and a later written self-evaluation of the teaching session.

Risk assessment

Ambitious scope

The lesson plan is deliberately ambitious: there are many frameworks and activities to cover in a single hour. It is likely that some parts will need to be abbreviated or skipped to finish on time.

The instructor should be prepared to prioritize core pieces—such as obstacles and one or two frameworks—if time runs short.

Continuity

Connection to other lessons

This lesson builds on ideas from Jason’s lesson on strategic learners, particularly the metacognitive skills required to set and pursue learning goals. The focus on cognitive development and thinking frameworks sets the stage for future lessons on active learning and other pedagogical strategies.

Analysis of how well the lesson met its goals, and what should change next time, is provided in a separate teaching self-evaluation.
